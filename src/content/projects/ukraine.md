We have a special relationship with our medical colleagues in Ukraine.

On 10 January 2022, we chartered our first branch in Ukraine with 16 charter members. We were in discussion with them about joint projects and exchange visits and many other exciting opportunities for collaboration.

The world changed on 24th February 2022 when Russia invaded Ukraine.

At a meeting on 5 March 2022 attended by 80 people we decided to initiate medical aid for Ukraine.

Since the onset of the war four years ago, we have stood firmly beside our colleagues in Ukraine. Our support has taken many forms — medical, humanitarian and logistical — totalling over £705,000 to date. The table below summarises our direct contributions.

Over the past two years, we have concentrated our efforts on a low-cost, high-impact intervention: supplying Kangaroo Mother Care (KMC) carriers to the Institute of Neonatology in Kyiv. This simple, evidence-based method is saving newborn lives every day, particularly in a health system strained by conflict and infrastructure disruption.

## Kangaroo Mother Care in Ukraine

From the beginning of the war the Fellowship has mobilised significant medical aid to Ukraine. After 18 months of emergency medical aid, we made a strategic decision to support maternity care – focusing mainly on babies born with a low birth weight. We concluded that the most effective way to give newborn babies the best chance of survival is Kangaroo Mother Care (KMC).

KMC involves keeping small (preterm) babies skin-to-skin with their mother (or another family member), with exclusive breast or breast-milk feeding and medical assessment. Studies of KMC report increased survival, decreased infection, improved nutrition and growth and possible improved development. Its benefits extend far beyond the well-known advantages of skin-to-skin care, and the support it has provided to perinatal facilities in Ukraine has exceeded all expectations.

In Ukraine, babies are born into a terrifying world of war. Newborn babies are spending the first days of their lives in shelters and basements (UNICEF). KMC is a simple, life-saving tool.

### A vital device for mums in areas of conflict

During active hostilities, the number of premature births increases, and the lack of electricity complicates the uninterrupted operation of equipment in neonatal wards. Because of unpredictable bombings, mothers must run to bomb shelters in haste. With the help of Kangaroo carriers, we can increase the safety of both mothers and babies, even in these extreme circumstances.

In addition to the carriers themselves, a large part of the project is dedicated to training healthcare professionals – as well as parents of premature babies – how to implement Kangaroo Mother Care effectively, to ensure the most effective and efficient care.

The carriers are manufactured in Moldova by Ascend Group SRL (Director Geta Rasciuc – a sling and carrier expert with over 17 years of experience and a board member of the International Council of Babywearing Consulting Schools), using local materials. They are made in accordance with the EU safety standard EN 13209-1, using materials that meet the clinical hygiene requirements for washing at high temperatures.

The Fellowship has supplied 8,000 KMC carriers to neonatal units in Ukraine and has received excellent feedback from medical and nursing staff as well as mums and dads.

## Protecting pregnant women and newborns

The war poses both immediate and long-term risks to mothers, fathers and new-borns – among them premature births, which can lead to a host of complications both immediately and later in life. Colleagues from Ukraine told us of a high rate of Caesarean sections and a higher than normal number of children born with birth asphyxia (oxygen starvation). There were also several reports of direct attacks on health facilities and ambulances, and disruption to supply chains.

Around May 2022, we became concerned about the impact of the war on pregnant women and the new-born, and prepared a programme to support 37 maternity departments. The Fellowship has supplied vital maternity care medical equipment to these hospitals, to help pregnant women give birth in relative safety and with better care.

It is estimated that these 37 hospitals will see about 30,000 births in the next 12 months. It is our hope that the vital supplies now available to these specialists will save lives.

## Hats for Ukrainian Babies

Often considered an adornment, hats for new-born babies may be life-saving. Their heads are relatively larger than their bodies compared with older children and adults, and a large source of heat loss. Mild heat loss may be associated with poor feeding and growth; larger losses with decreased body temperature and an increased chance of death.

The hats are often knitted – especially, but not exclusively, by grandmothers – sometimes along with booties and mittens. We have supplied 10,000 knitted hats and a large quantity of baby clothes to maternity units in Ukraine, thanks to the generosity of UK Rotarians and friends. (We are no longer able to collect or transport such items to Ukraine.)

## Helping babies breathe – Neopuff

NEOPUFF is a simple device to help babies breathe; in many cases, having it in the right place at the right time makes the difference between life and death. In partnership with the Rotary Club of Klosterneuburg and with the support of RC Leoben, the Fellowship delivered 25 Neopuff devices to 15 cities in Ukraine.

> "Such a device as Neopuff can literally save the future of Ukraine by saving newly born, little babies… you are a part of a big important project and we value it a lot!" — Yuliya Bitner, Lviv
